# PubMed Literature Analysis: FDA MAUDE Database Mining (Past 10 Years)

> [!NOTE]
> This report summarizes PubMed literature from the past 10 years (2016-2026) concerning data mining, text mining, natural language processing (NLP), machine learning (ML), and Large Language Models (LLMs) applied to the FDA's Manufacturer and User Facility Device Experience (MAUDE) database.

## Overview of Findings

We identified **41** relevant papers published between 2016 and 2026. The research can be broadly categorized into three major areas:

1. **Large Language Models & Generative AI (8 papers):** The most recent trend (2024-2026) leverages models like GPT-4 to read unstructured narrative descriptions in MAUDE reports, perform Information Extraction (ETL), build interactive safety chatbots, and automate research question formulation.
2. **Traditional NLP & Machine Learning (29 papers):** Applying classifiers (random forest, SVM, deep learning, BERT) to categorize safety events, predict device recalls, identify misclassified deaths, and analyze health IT adverse events.
3. **Clinical Device Safety and Advanced Statistical Mining (4 papers):** Large-scale cross-sectional studies that develop specific classification schemes or conduct statistical text-mining to compare complication rates of specific medical devices.

### 1. Automating Safety Surveillance for Software-Based Medical Devices: Insights from FDA MAUDE Data.
- **Journal & Date:** Stud Health Technol Inform (2026 May 21)
- **PMID:** [42174919](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42174919)
- **Authors:** Kesharwani R, Cvijic L, Denecke K
- **Matched Keywords:** `natural language processing, classification, large language model, clustering`
- **Key Abstract Details:** Digital health interventions (DHIs) use digital technologies to deliver and support healthcare. When registered as medical devices incidents associated with them have to be reported. This study examines errors and incidents involving software-based medical devices as recorded in the U.S. Food and Drug Administration's MAUDE database. Using natural language processing methods, specifically BERTopic clustering and large language model-based classification, we analysed narrative reports to identify event types, contributing factors and temporal patterns. Themes that emerged related to cybersecurity, patient-device communication, and pandemic monitoring suggest that post-market surveillance is expanding to encompass broader human-device and public health contexts. While MAUDE narratives offer valuable insights, their limited contextual detail restricts root-cause analysis. Enhanced reporting structures are needed to support the safer design and oversight of DHIs.
